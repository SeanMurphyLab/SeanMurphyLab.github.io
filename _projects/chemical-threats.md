Chemical threat agents pose a serious risk to respiratory health in both civilian and military settings, yet our understanding of how these agents injure the human airway remains limited. Existing knowledge relies heavily on animal studies, which often fail to capture the cellular and molecular responses unique to human tissue, leaving critical gaps in the development of effective medical countermeasures.

With support from the National Institutes of Health Countermeasures Against Chemical Threats (CounterACT) program, our lab is using a human airway-on-a-chip microphysiological system to model the effects of chemical threat agent exposure with greater human relevance than traditional animal models. The platform recreates the architecture of the human airway, including a multi-cell-type epithelial layer, a stromal compartment, and a microvascular endothelium, all exposed to physiologically realistic airflow and fluid circulation. A custom exposure system allows precise control over the concentration and duration of gas delivery to the airway tissue.

Using this system, we are characterizing the chemical, biological, structural, and functional consequences of chemical threat exposure across a range of doses and timepoints. Transcriptomic and metabolomic analyses are being used to identify the molecular pathways driving acute airway injury, providing insight into mechanisms of toxicity that are difficult to study in human subjects. These findings will help define the time course of injury after exposure and identify potential targets for therapeutic intervention.

This work supports the broader CounterACT mission of developing medical countermeasures against chemical threats, while reducing reliance on animal testing through the use of human-relevant in vitro models. By advancing our understanding of chemical threat agent toxicity at the cellular and molecular level, this research aims to inform strategies for protecting and treating individuals exposed to chemical threats in the future.
