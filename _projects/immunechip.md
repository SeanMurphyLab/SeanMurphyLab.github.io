Developing vaccines and therapeutics against pandemic and chemical, biological, radiological, and nuclear (CBRN) threats is slow and resource-intensive, and animal models often predict human responses poorly. The ImmuneChip+ program, supported by a five-year, $18 million BARDA award through its Division of Research, Innovation, and Ventures (DRIVe), addresses this bottleneck using microphysiological systems (MPS), or "tissue chips," that recreate functional human tissues in miniature, three-dimensional form.

At WFIRM, we are building an integrated tissue-chip platform that reconstructs the human immune response to vaccination on a single device. ImmuneChip+ connects four organ tissue equivalents (OTEs), representing muscle, lymph node, bone marrow, and airway tissues, that together capture the sequential stages of immunity: the vaccine injection site, antigen processing and presentation, activation and clonal expansion of adaptive immune cells, antibody production, and neutralization of virus at an infected lung tissue. The bone marrow module models long-lived memory, allowing antigen re-challenge to confirm durable protection.

What makes the platform distinctive is its inclusion of both cellular and humoral immune components in physiologically relevant 3D tissue, enabling non-clinical testing of vaccine efficacy over extended timeframes. WFIRM has previously sustained these tissue models for up to six months, a feature critical for measuring the strength and duration of immune responses such as neutralizing antibody production and memory B-cell formation.

As a proof of concept, the platform reproduces the immune response elicited by an FDA-approved seasonal influenza A vaccine. By capturing the full cascade of immunity in human tissue, and by generating data rapidly without the need for clinical trials, ImmuneChip+ offers a precise route to evaluate vaccines and to accelerate countermeasure development for pandemic preparedness and response.
