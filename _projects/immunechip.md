Developing vaccines and therapeutics against pandemic and chemical, biological, radiological, and nuclear (CBRN) threats is slow and resource-intensive, and animal models often predict human responses poorly. The ImmuneChip+ program, supported by BARDA, addresses this bottleneck using microphysiological systems (MPS), or "tissue chips," that recreate functional human tissues in miniature, three-dimensional form.

At WFIRM, we are building an integrated tissue-chip platform that reconstructs the human immune response to vaccination on a single device. Four connected organ tissue equivalents (OTEs), representing muscle, lymph node, bone marrow, and airway tissues, capture the sequential stages of immunity: the vaccine injection site, antigen processing and presentation, activation of adaptive immune cells, antibody production, and neutralization of virus at an infected lung tissue. The bone marrow module models long-lived memory, allowing antigen re-challenge to confirm durable protection.

As a proof of concept, the platform reproduces the immune response elicited by an FDA-approved seasonal influenza A vaccine. By capturing the full cascade of cellular and antibody-mediated immunity in human tissue, ImmuneChip+ offers a precise, non-clinical route to evaluate vaccine efficacy, accelerating countermeasure development against emerging infectious threats.

For more information, see the [BARDA DRIVe project portfolio](https://drive.hhs.gov/portfolio.html?id=Wake%20Forest%20Institute%20for%20Regenerative%20Medicine).
